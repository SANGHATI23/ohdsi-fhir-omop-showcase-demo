Phase 4 Analytical Stability Evaluation — Results Draft

We evaluated analytical stability across five OMOP databases generated from controlled FHIR perturbation variants. The clean V0 baseline was compared against four perturbed variants: V1_missing_demographics, V2_duplicate_encounter_ids, V3_conflicting_codings, and V4_missing_medications.

Across all variants, structural row counts remained stable, with 1,071 persons and 25,000 rows each in visit_occurrence, condition_occurrence, drug_exposure, measurement, observation, and procedure_occurrence. This indicates that row-count preservation alone was insufficient to detect downstream analytical differences.

V1_missing_demographics produced measurable demographic drift. Specifically, 161 of 1,071 persons had missing or unknown gender and missing or zero birth year, corresponding to 15.03% demographic missingness. In contrast, V0, V2, V3, and V4 preserved complete demographic fields. This demonstrates that source-level missing demographic attributes were retained after FHIR-to-OMOP transformation and remained detectable in OMOP person-level attributes.

V2_duplicate_encounter_ids did not create duplicate OMOP visit_occurrence_id values. Inspection of visit_occurrence showed that OMOP visit_occurrence_id values were regenerated as sequential unique integer identifiers, while visit_source_value represented visit categories such as AMB, EMER, IMP, HH, and VR rather than the original FHIR Encounter.id. Therefore, duplicate source Encounter identifiers were hidden by the transformation process instead of producing visible row-count divergence. This suggests a traceability limitation: source-level identity defects may become analytically invisible after transformation.

V3_conflicting_codings increased source-level semantic heterogeneity. The number of unique condition_source_value entries increased from 247 in V0 to 412 in V3, with 170 new source-value patterns introduced. Many new values combined source codes with E11.9, indicating that the conflicting coding perturbation was preserved in the OMOP source-value layer. However, all variants had 100% of condition_occurrence rows mapped to condition_concept_id = 0, indicating that standardized condition concept mapping was not resolved. Thus, V3 produced source-level semantic drift without corresponding standardized OMOP concept preservation.

V4_missing_medications did not reduce the total number of drug_exposure rows, which remained 25,000 across all variants. However, V4 altered medication completeness patterns. Persons with drug exposure increased from 609 in V0 to 666 in V4, unique drug_source_value entries increased from 146 to 150, and missing drug_source_value rows increased from 9,687 to 9,843. This indicates that missing medication resources did not manifest as simple row loss, but instead produced distributional drift in person-level drug exposure assignment and drug source-value completeness.

Overall, these results show that structurally stable OMOP outputs can still contain meaningful analytical differences across demographic, identity, semantic, and medication exposure dimensions. The corrected analytical divergence heatmap summarizes these variant-specific effects and demonstrates that row-count validation alone is insufficient for evaluating the reliability of FHIR-to-OMOP transformation pipelines.
